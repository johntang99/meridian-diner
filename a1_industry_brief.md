## Section 1: Customer Discovery Journey

### Fine Dining

**How customers discover:**
1. **Google Search** ("best fine dining [city]", "tasting menu [city]") — primary for out-of-towners
2. **Word of mouth / social circles** — dominant for locals; trust comes from peer recommendations
3. **Instagram** — browsed for ambiance validation after discovery; rarely the entry point
4. **OpenTable/Resy editorial lists** — "Best New Restaurants", "James Beard nominees"
5. **Press coverage** — NYT, Eater, Infatuation reviews drive significant traffic spikes

**What they look at first:**
1. Photography — specifically ambiance shots (is it appropriate for the occasion?)
2. Menu — price range and cuisine style (am I being surprised or reassured?)
3. Reservation availability — is there a table or not?
4. Reviews/press mentions — third-party validation
5. About/Chef page — only after deciding to explore further

**Top 3 friction points:**
1. No online reservation system or broken embed → they leave and book somewhere else
2. PDF menu (non-scannable on mobile, no SEO) → can't quickly assess price range
3. Photos that undersell the actual experience → under-promising loses bookings

**Trust signals that close the decision:**
- Real photography (not stock) of both food AND dining room
- Press logos (Michelin, James Beard, Eater, NYT) shown prominently
- Chef biography with verifiable credentials
- Visible reservation availability (seeing "7:30pm — 2 available" creates urgency)
- Specific, named dishes with descriptions (signals culinary confidence)

**Cultural differences:**
- **Chinese-speaking guests:** Research extensively before visiting; trust peer-reviewed platforms (Yelp, Dianping) over press coverage; photos of specific dishes matter more than ambiance; language-switched pages dramatically increase conversion
- **Spanish-speaking guests:** Rely heavily on community word-of-mouth; respond well to warmth in copy tone; phone number visibility is more important (higher phone reservation rate)

### Cafe / Brunch

**How customers discover:**
1. **Google Maps** ("brunch near me") — majority of discovery
2. **Instagram** — extremely high impact; aesthetic and specific dishes go viral
3. **TikTok** — "best brunch in [city]" content drives direct visit intent
4. **Yelp** — still significant for cafe/casual, especially 25–40 age group
5. **Neighborhood blogs / local influencers** — high conversion

**What they look at first:**
1. Photos of specific food items (avocado toast, eggs benny, latte art)
2. Hours — are they open right now / this weekend?
3. Address and parking
4. Wait time information (if available)
5. Menu prices

**Top 3 friction points:**
1. Hours not prominently displayed or outdated → lost walkin + planned visits
2. Menu with no photos for a visual-forward concept → reduces confidence
3. No sense of wait/availability (weekend brunch lines are a real concern)

**Trust signals:**
- "Open Now" indicator (reduces friction dramatically)
- Instagram-worthy food photography for specific hero items
- Neighborhood credibility ("Serving Carroll Gardens since 2018")
- Google rating with count (4.7 ★ with 800+ reviews beats 4.9 with 40)
- Real photos of the actual space (not renders or stock)

**Cultural differences:**
- **Chinese-speaking guests:** Very photo-driven for cafes; specific dish names in Chinese increase order confidence; community WeChat groups drive referrals — a Chinese-language page screenshot gets shared
- **Spanish-speaking guests:** Value family-friendliness signals; bilingual menu increases comfort; testimonials in Spanish are highly trusted

---

### Bar / Lounge

**How customers discover:**
1. **Google Search** ("cocktail bar [neighborhood]", "best bars [city]")
2. **Instagram** — cocktail photography and moody interior shots drive strong discovery
3. **Yelp** — important for reputation management
4. **Nightlife guides** (Time Out, Eater, local guides)
5. **Word of mouth** — still dominant for local regulars

**What they look at first:**
1. Vibe photography — is this the right atmosphere?
2. Hours (especially late-night hours — is it open at 11pm?)
3. Cocktail menu — signature drinks signal identity
4. Reservation policy (walk-in only? Required? Weekend only?)
5. Events (live music, DJ nights)

**Top 3 friction points:**
1. Unclear hours (especially if they stay open late) → missed late-night visits
2. No indication of music/noise level → wrong crowd expectations
3. Reservation confusion (when required vs. walk-in only) → no-shows and lost tables

**Trust signals:**
- Press coverage (Time Out, Eater, "Best Of" lists)
- Cocktail menu with genuine descriptions (not generic)
- Photos that authentically show crowd density and lighting
- Named bartenders / opening team (craft bars build on personality)
- Events calendar (signals an active, curated space)

## Section 2: Competitor Audit

### Fine Dining

**Eleven Madison Park (elevenmadisonpark.com)**
- ✅ What they do brilliantly: Minimalist hero with a single, perfect seasonal image. Reservation widget is the only CTA — no noise. Typography uses extreme white space to signal premium.
- ❌ What's missing: No chef biography on homepage despite Daniel Humm being a major draw. Mobile experience feels afterthought — hero crops poorly.
- 💡 Steal: Full-viewport seasonal image that changes quarterly. Discipline to have ONE CTA.

**Le Bernardin (le-bernardin.com)**
- ✅ What they do brilliantly: Press logos above the fold (Michelin 3-star, NYT 4-star) immediately establish authority. Chef Eric Ripert's photo and bio prominent — personal brand enhances restaurant trust.
- ❌ What's missing: Menu is difficult to navigate on mobile. Gallery feels dated — grid layout, no lightbox.
- 💡 Steal: Press/awards placement above the fold on homepage, not buried.

**Alinea (alinearestaurant.com)**
- ✅ What they do brilliantly: Ticketing model shown clearly — their unusual reservation-as-ticket system is explained, not hidden. Photography is editorial and unexpected (abstract food art).
- ❌ What's missing: Site speed is poor. Concept explanation is buried — first-timers are confused.
- 💡 Steal: Don't hide unusual policies — explain them upfront to build trust rather than erode it.

## Section 3: Mobile-First Realities

Restaurant sites average **68–74% mobile traffic**. The failures are consistent and avoidable:

### Top 5 Mobile UX Failures

**Failure 1: PDF Menus**
- PDFs require pinch-zoom, can't be indexed by Google, don't adapt to screen size
- Solution: HTML menus with large touch targets (44px min), category sticky nav, readable at 16px minimum
- Impact: PDF menus lose an estimated 30–40% of mobile visitors before they see prices

**Failure 2: Reservation Widgets That Break on Mobile**
- OpenTable embeds with tiny date pickers designed for desktop
- Solution: Use OpenTable's mobile-optimized embed, or custom form with native date input (`<input type="date">`)
- Critical: Test on actual iOS Safari and Android Chrome — not just desktop responsive view

**Failure 3: Hero Images That Crop Badly**
- Desktop landscape hero image: crops to a confusing detail on mobile portrait
- Solution: Use `object-position` to control crop point; provide a separate mobile image (1:1 or 4:5 ratio)
- Better: Design hero with centered subject and text in safe zone that works at all ratios

**Failure 4: Slow Image Loading**
- Restaurant sites are image-heavy; unoptimized images cause 8–15 second loads
- Solution: Next.js Image with WebP conversion, proper srcset, lazy loading below fold
- Targets: Hero image < 200KB, LCP < 2.5s, total page weight < 1.5MB

**Failure 5: Phone Number Not Clickable**
- A restaurant's most important conversion tool for mobile is click-to-call
- Solution: `<a href="tel:+1XXXXXXXXXX">` on every phone number, every page
- Bonus: Sticky bottom bar on mobile with phone + reservation CTA

### Click-to-Call Placement Best Practices
- Header: small phone icon / number — always visible
- Footer: full number with click-to-call
- Contact page: large, prominent, above fold
- Sticky mobile bar: phone + "Reserve" as two equal CTAs

## Section 5: Reservation & Conversion Patterns

### OpenTable vs Resy vs Custom Form

| Factor | OpenTable | Resy | Custom Form |
|---|---|---|---|
| Best for | High-volume, established restaurants | Upscale/boutique, discovery-oriented | Small/independent, full control |
| Discovery benefit | Yes — OT marketplace drives new diners | Yes — Resy editorial drives press-adjacent discovery | None |
| Cost | Per-cover fees ($1–$1.50/cover) | Subscription + lower per-cover | One-time build cost only |
| Customization | Limited | Moderate | Full |
| Mobile UX | Good (modern embed) | Better (cleaner UI) | Depends on implementation |
| Recommended for | Any restaurant wanting discovery + volume | Fine dining, chef-driven concepts | Cafes, fixed-price only, or small operators |

**Recommendation per sub-type:**
- Fine Dining → Resy (premium positioning, editorial presence)
- Cafe/Brunch → Custom form or none (walk-in dominant)
- Bar/Lounge → OpenTable or custom (depends on volume and cover model)

### Reservation CTA Placement
- **Homepage hero:** Primary CTA — "Reserve a Table" button visible without scrolling
- **Sticky header:** Persistent "Reserve" button on all pages (desktop)
- **Sticky mobile bottom bar:** "Reserve" + phone number as equal CTAs
- **Menu page:** CTA after menu preview — "Ready to visit? Reserve your table"
- **About/Chef page:** CTA at bottom — "Experience our menu in person"
- **Contact page:** Reservation widget embedded, not just a link

### Copy That Converts
- ✅ "Reserve a Table" — clear, direct
- ✅ "Book Your Evening" — aspirational for fine dining
- ✅ "Check Availability" — low-commitment framing
- ❌ "Make a Reservation" — bureaucratic, cold
- ❌ "Click Here to Book" — generic

### Urgency Messaging
- "Only 3 tables remaining tonight" → 18–22% lift in same-day conversions (OpenTable internal data)
- "Fully booked — join waitlist" → captures intent that would otherwise be lost
- "Next available: Friday at 7:30pm" → removes friction by surfacing availability proactively

## Section 6: SEO Landscape

### Critical On-Page SEO for Restaurant Sites

**Technical requirements:**
- `<title>` pattern: `{Dish or Service} in {City} | {Restaurant Name}` — for service pages
- Meta description: include cuisine type, neighborhood, signature experience (160 chars max)
- `canonical` tags on all multilingual pages
- `hreflang` tags across all locale versions
- Sitemap.xml submitted to Google Search Console
- `robots.txt` allowing all key pages

**Content requirements:**
- Neighborhood name in H1 and first paragraph on contact/about pages
- Service area pages if multi-location ("Fine Dining in Midtown Manhattan")
- Blog/editorial content targeting "best [cuisine] in [city]" head terms

### Schema.org Types for Restaurants

```json
{
  "@type": "Restaurant",
  "name": "Restaurant Name",
  "address": { "@type": "PostalAddress", ... },
  "telephone": "+1-XXX-XXX-XXXX",
  "openingHoursSpecification": [...],
  "servesCuisine": "Contemporary American",
  "priceRange": "$$$$",
  "hasMenu": "https://example.com/en/menu",
  "acceptsReservations": true,
  "aggregateRating": { "@type": "AggregateRating", "ratingValue": "4.8", "reviewCount": "324" }
}
```

Additional schemas:
- `MenuItem` — on menu pages, each item with name, description, price
- `Event` — on events pages with startDate, endDate, price, location
- `BlogPosting` — on every blog article
- `BreadcrumbList` — on all pages (boosts click-through rate in SERPs)

### Multilingual Pages and Local SEO
- Each locale version (`/en/`, `/zh/`, `/es/`) is treated as a separate page by Google
- A Chinese-language page can rank in Google for queries from Chinese-speaking searchers
- `hreflang="zh-Hans"` for Simplified Chinese, `hreflang="es"` for Spanish
- Do NOT use Google Translate auto-translation — Google penalizes this; human/AI quality translation required

### Common SEO Failures
1. PDF menu (not crawlable — Google can't read your dishes)
2. No Google My Business profile linked or maintained
3. Duplicate content across locales without `hreflang`
4. Missing schema markup (restaurants without schema lose rich results)
5. JavaScript-only rendered content (Next.js SSR/ISR solves this)

## Section 7: Premium Design Patterns — 10 Differentiators

### 1. Editorial Typography as Identity
Premium sites use display fonts with extreme intentionality — wide letter-spacing on fine dining, warm weight on bistros. Generic sites use Inter or Roboto at default weight. The font choice communicates the restaurant's personality before a single word is read.

### 2. Full-Bleed, Art-Directed Photography
Premium: photographer-shot images with consistent color grading, controlled lighting, specific framing. Budget: stock photography or phone snapshots. The difference is immediately legible to any visitor.

### 3. White Space as a Luxury Signal
Fine dining sites use generous padding that communicates "we are not in a hurry." A menu item with 40px padding around it reads as premium. The same item at 12px reads as a diner menu.

### 4. Intentional Color Restraint
Premium restaurant sites use 2–3 colors maximum, applied with discipline. The background might be near-black, a single gold accent, and white text. Budget sites use 6+ colors and gradients that signal template thinking.

### 5. Hover Interactions That Feel Considered
A menu item that lifts 4px with a 200ms ease-out on hover signals craft. A button that blinks or has a jarring color swap signals a template. Motion should feel like the restaurant itself — smooth, unhurried.

### 6. The Chef as Character
Premium sites introduce the chef as a person — a photo that feels real, a bio written in first person, perhaps a quote about the philosophy. This humanizes the brand and builds emotional investment before the first visit.

### 7. Seasonal Awareness Built In
A restaurant site that shows "Spring Menu 2026" signals that someone is tending to it. Outdated "Fall 2023" menus signal neglect. The visual system should make seasonal updates trivially easy — a badge component, a swap of the hero image.

### 8. Mobile Navigation That Respects Context
Premium mobile nav: full-screen overlay with large touch targets, reservation CTA always visible. Budget: tiny hamburger menu with links that require precise tapping, CTA buried.

### 9. Consistency Across Every Touchpoint
Font, color, photography style, and copy tone are identical whether you're on the menu page, the about page, or the 404. Budget sites inherit inconsistent styles page-to-page.

### 10. Purposeful Negative Space in Photography
The best restaurant photography has the dish isolated, breathing room, a considered background. Budget photography fills every pixel. Negative space in photography communicates the same thing as white space in layout — confidence.
